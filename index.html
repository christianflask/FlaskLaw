About the Firm

Flask Law, PLLC

Christian Flask founded Flask Law to fill a specific gap: legal counsel that understands both sides of the contract — the regulatory environment that shapes it and the operational reality that has to live with it.

Before practicing law, he spent nearly two decades as a software engineer and cyberspace operations professional, working at the intersection of technology, national security, and federal procurement. That background informs everything. Where many attorneys see a contract, he sees the compliance architecture behind it — the FAR clauses, the data handling obligations, the risk that doesn’t show up in the recitals but surfaces eighteen months into performance.

He has served as legal counsel in some of the most demanding environments in federal service, including as Associate Counsel at the Naval Information Warfare Center Atlantic and as General Counsel at NATO’s Allied Command Counterintelligence, where he managed intelligence oversight programs and contributed to the development of NATO’s data protection policy framework.

Flask Law is a private-side advisory practice. The firm counsels businesses and professionals operating in regulated, complex, and national-security-adjacent environments — on contracts, compliance, governance, and risk — without representing clients before federal agencies or in matters directly involving the United States Government.

If your business operates where the rules are complicated and the stakes for getting it wrong are real, that’s where this firm works best.

Practice Areas

Where the Firm Focuses

Flask Law advises businesses and professionals operating in regulated, technology-driven, and defense-adjacent environments. The firm’s focus areas reflect a deliberate combination of legal expertise and operational experience — not a general practice spread thin.

01

Contract Advisory

Contracts define the relationship. They also define the exposure. Flask Law advises businesses and professionals on the full lifecycle of commercial contracts — from initial structure and negotiation through execution and risk review. The firm focuses on agreements where the underlying regulatory environment matters: teaming agreements between private parties, subcontractor and vendor agreements, NDAs, professional services agreements, and other commercial arrangements common in technology and defense-adjacent industries. The goal is not just a clean document — it is a contract that reflects what you actually agreed to, allocates risk deliberately, and holds up when it needs to.

02

Compliance & Risk Management

Compliance is not a checklist. It is a framework your business either has or doesn’t — and the difference shows up in your contracts, your operations, and your exposure. Flask Law provides legal advisory services on internal governance, policy development, contract risk mitigation, and regulatory compliance for businesses operating in complex and regulated environments. For companies that handle sensitive data, operate in regulated industries, or contract with parties that impose compliance obligations downstream, getting the legal foundation right is not optional. Flask Law helps clients understand what those obligations are, how they interact with existing contracts and operations, and how to structure internal frameworks that hold up under scrutiny.

03

Business Formation & Governance

The decisions made at formation — entity type, ownership structure, operating agreements, governance framework — shape everything that comes after. Fixing them later is expensive. Getting them right at the start is not. Flask Law advises founders and early-stage companies on entity formation, operating agreements, and corporate governance, with particular experience counseling businesses in technology, defense contracting, and other regulated sectors. For startups entering complex markets, having legally sound foundational documents is part of being taken seriously by partners, customers, and investors.

04

General Business & Commercial Matters

Flask Law handles select general business and commercial legal matters outside its primary practice areas on a case-by-case basis, including private real estate transactions and general commercial agreements. Inquire directly to discuss whether your matter is one the firm can assist with.

8(a) Program Suspensions: What Affected Firms Need to Know

Christian Flask, Esq. — Flask Law, PLLC

In January 2026, the Small Business Administration suspended more than 1,000 firms from the 8(a) Business Development Program — roughly one in four active participants — for alleged non-compliance with a December 2025 data call requiring three years of financial and contractual records. Since then, SBA has moved to terminate an additional 154 firms, and independent audits by the Department of Defense, Treasury, and GSA are underway.

If your firm received a Notice of Suspension, the situation is serious. But it is not necessarily permanent, and how you respond in the next several weeks matters.

What the Suspension Means Immediately

A suspended 8(a) firm cannot receive new competitive or sole-source 8(a) awards. Your firm is, however, required to continue performing on existing 8(a) contracts, and agencies may exercise options on those contracts during the suspension period. The suspension does not automatically affect your other government contracting activity outside the 8(a) program — but that distinction requires careful review of your specific contract portfolio.

Why Some Firms Were Suspended Despite Good-Faith Efforts

SBA took a strict approach to the January 19 deadline. Firms that submitted complete responses one day late — including some whose delays were caused by errors in the government-operated MySBA Certifications portal — received suspension notices regardless. SBA has indicated that the portal remained open for data call submissions until February 19, 2026, but it has not explicitly stated that late submissions will result in reinstatement. If you submitted data after the deadline, document that submission carefully and preserve all records of any portal errors you encountered.

Your Options

Suspended firms have two primary paths. The first is an informal request to SBA for reinstatement. This involves explaining the reason for the delay or deficiency, demonstrating that the firm continues to operate as a responsible contractor, and making the case that suspension is not warranted given the circumstances. For firms whose non-compliance was technical rather than substantive — portal errors, incomplete but good-faith submissions — this is often the appropriate first step.

The second is a formal appeal to SBA’s Office of Hearings and Appeals within 45 days of the date on your Notice of Suspension. This is a hard deadline. Missing it forecloses formal appeal rights. OHA proceedings are time-consuming and outcomes are not guaranteed, but for firms with substantial 8(a) contract pipelines at stake, the appeal process may be worth pursuing.

These are not mutually exclusive. Firms can pursue informal reinstatement while preserving formal appeal rights.

What You Should Be Doing Right Now

Regardless of which path you pursue, the immediate priority is documentation and internal review. That means gathering and organizing the records SBA requested — financial statements, employee lists, contract documentation — so you understand exactly what you submitted, what may have been missing, and what your compliance posture actually looks like before you make representations to SBA.

It also means reviewing your 8(a) eligibility status independently. The broader audit context suggests SBA is scrutinizing not just data call compliance but underlying eligibility — net worth thresholds, total assets, performance of work requirements, and subcontracting arrangements. Firms that have continued to participate in the 8(a) program as their financial position changed should assess whether their current status is defensible under the program’s eligibility standards.

The Broader Context

These suspensions are part of a significant restructuring of the 8(a) program under the current administration. SBA has formally eliminated race-based presumptions of social disadvantage, dramatically reduced new certifications, and signaled ongoing scrutiny of existing participants. The DoD audit includes a line-by-line review of sole-source and set-aside awards above $20 million, with particular focus on pass-through arrangements and subcontracting compliance.

For firms that clear the current suspension, the compliance environment going forward will be materially different from what it was two years ago. Internal governance, documentation practices, and contract performance oversight are no longer just administrative requirements — they are survival requirements for continued 8(a) participation.
